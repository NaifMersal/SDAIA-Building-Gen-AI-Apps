A. From Words to Intelligence

A conceptual tour of the most important ideas.

It Started with Word Completion

The original goal was simple: predict the next word.

Statistical Language Models (N-Grams)

*‚ÄúThe capital of Saudi Arabia is ___‚Äú*

  • Riyadh (99.8%) ‚úÖ - Jeddah (0.1%) - Paris (0.00001%)

The model looked at the words before it and counted: what word most often follows ‚Äúcapital of Saudi Arabia is‚Äù in our corpus?

The Model as a Function

Think of it this way, based on the data we have (corpus), the model is just a function:

\[f(\text{sentence}) = \frac{\text{count}(\text{sentence} + \text{next_word})}{\text{count}(\text{sentence})}\]

Input: a sentence. Output: the most likely next word based purely on counting.

The question became: can we build a better function ‚Äî one that understands words, not just counts them?

Evolution: Step 1 ‚Äî Word Completion

The machine learned to finish sentences ‚Äî nothing more.

Words Got Meaning ‚Äî Neural LMs

Remember our function \(f(\text{sentence}) \rightarrow \text{next_word}\)?

A neural network learned to represent words as vectors ‚Äî coordinates in a space where meaning is geometry, not counting.

Same Function, Better Implementation

Statistical LMs: \(f\) = count frequencies ‚Üí pick the most common next word

Neural LMs: \(f\) = understand meaning ‚Üí pick the word that makes sense next

Why Vectors?

When words are coordinates, similarity becomes distance.

  • ‚Äúking‚Äù and ‚Äúqueen‚Äù land near each other
  • The Arabic and English words for ‚Äúhouse‚Äù are neighbors
  • Cross-lingual translation emerged naturally, the model operates on meaning, not spelling

Coming up in the RAG module

We‚Äôll dive deep into how these vectors work ‚Äî and how we use them to build retrieval systems.

Evolution: Step 2 ‚Äî Meaning Enters

Same task. Richer representation. The machine finally understood relationships between words.

The Architecture Shift: Transformers (2017)

Before 2017, models read text sequentially ‚Äî word by word, left to right. (LSTMs)

The Transformer (‚ÄúAttention is All You Need‚Äù, Google 2017) changed everything:

  • Parallel processing: Read the entire document at once
  • Self-Attention: Every word looks at every other word to find relevance
  • Eliminated the bottleneck: Now we could finally train on internet-scale data

Why It Mattered

This is the ‚Äúengine‚Äù inside every modern model ‚Äî GPT, Claude, Gemini, Llama. Without it, scale was impossible.

The Scale Breakthrough ‚Äî GPT & LLMs (2018‚Äì2020)

Once we had the Transformer, we just needed to scale it up.

  • GPT-1 (2018) proved it worked.
  • GPT-2 (2019) proved it could write believable essays.
  • GPT-3 (2020) proved that Scale is a quality of its own.

The Revelation

At 175B parameters, GPT-3 could translate, summarize, write code, and answer questions ‚Äî without being explicitly trained for any of them.

The task never changed. The scale did ‚Äî and scale changed everything.

C. The ChatGPT Revolution

How SFT turned completers into assistants

The Problem with Raw Completers

Base GPT-2: ‚ÄúWrite me a poem‚Äù

Write me a poem about the weather
in a different language. Write me a
poem about the weather in a...

It continues the sentence. It doesn‚Äôt understand you issued a command.

ChatGPT: ‚ÄúWrite me a poem‚Äù

Here's a poem for you:

The morning light breaks soft and low,
Across the hills where rivers flow...

It treats your text as an instruction and responds accordingly.

What changed?

Not the underlying completion mechanism ‚Äî the training data and process.

Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)

Human annotators wrote thousands of correct instruction-following responses. The model was then trained to mimic them.

[Prompt]   ‚Üí "Summarize this article in 3 bullet points."
[Response] ‚Üí "‚Ä¢ Point 1\n‚Ä¢ Point 2\n‚Ä¢ Point 3"

[Prompt]   ‚Üí "Translate 'Good morning' to French."
[Response] ‚Üí "Bonjour"

The model learned a new pattern: prompts are commands, not text to continue.

Still completion under the hood

SFT didn‚Äôt change the architecture. It changed what the model learned to complete ‚Äî now it completes helpful assistant responses.

RLHF in 30 Seconds

After SFT, a second step aligned the model with human preferences:

  1. Generate multiple responses to the same prompt
  2. Human rankers pick which response is better
  3. Train a reward model on those preferences
  4. Use the reward model to further tune the base model

One-sentence intuition

RLHF is how we teach the model to prefer helpful and honest completions over merely plausible ones.

The Model Spectrum

Each step is an evolutionary refinement ‚Äî same foundation, more alignment.

D. In-Context Learning

No retraining required ‚Äî just better prompts

The Insight That Changed Everything

You don‚Äôt need to retrain the model. Just show it examples in the prompt.

GPT-3 (2020) demonstrated that a large enough model could adapt to a new task from a handful of examples ‚Äî with no gradient updates, no fine-tuning, no cost.

This was called In-Context Learning (ICL), and it unlocked prompt engineering as a discipline.

Zero-Shot vs Few-Shot

Zero-Shot

Classify the sentiment:
"The service was terrible."

Sentiment:

Model output: Negative

Works for simple, well-defined tasks.

Few-Shot

"Great product!" ‚Üí Positive
"Terrible service." ‚Üí Negative
"It was okay." ‚Üí Neutral

"Absolutely loved it!" ‚Üí

Model output: Positive

Works for nuanced or custom formats.

Rule of thumb

When zero-shot gives inconsistent results, add 2‚Äì3 examples. That‚Äôs usually enough.

The system prompt is the personality dial ‚Äî it shapes every response without the user seeing it.

  2. Structured format ‚Äî JSON, markdown, bullet points

E. Thinking Models

From chain of thought to o1 and beyond

‚ÄúLet‚Äôs Think Step by Step‚Äù

In 2022, researchers discovered a single phrase nearly doubled accuracy on math benchmarks:

Direct answer

Q: Roger has 5 tennis balls.
He buys 2 more cans of 3.
How many does he have?

A: 11

Wrong. (The model guessed.)

With Chain of Thought

Q: Roger has 5 tennis balls.
He buys 2 more cans of 3.
How many does he have?
Let's think step by step.

A: He starts with 5.
2 cans √ó 3 balls = 6 more.
5 + 6 = 11.

Correct ‚Äî and verifiable.

Why It Works

Forcing the model to generate intermediate steps means:

  • More tokens generated before the final answer
  • More ‚Äúcomputation‚Äù applied to the problem
  • Each step constrains what the next step can plausibly be

Intuition

The model doesn‚Äôt think before it writes ‚Äî it thinks by writing. More tokens = more thinking.

The Thinking Model Era

The shift: from prompting a model to think step-by-step, to training a model that reasons internally before responding.

The Trade-off

Standard Model Thinking Model
Response time ~1 second ~15‚Äì30 seconds
Cost Low 3‚Äì10√ó higher
Best for Chat, summarization, extraction Math, coding, complex reasoning
Failure mode Hallucination Overthinking simple tasks

When to use thinking models

Use them when correctness matters more than speed ‚Äî audit tasks, code generation, structured data extraction. Don‚Äôt use them for real-time chat.

E. Under the Hood

The Atomic Unit: Tokens

The machine doesn‚Äôt actually see ‚Äúwords.‚Äù It sees Tokens.

  • What are they? Fragments of words ‚Äî sometimes parts of a word, sometimes a whole word.
  • Why? It‚Äôs more efficient than having a dictionary of every possible word.
  • The Result: The model can read and write words it has never seen before by combining fragments.

Rule of Thumb

1,000 tokens ‚âà 750 words. When you‚Äôre billed for ‚Äúinput and output,‚Äù you‚Äôre paying for these atomic fragments.

The Stop Token

<EOS> (End of Sequence) is a special token in the vocabulary. When the model picks it, generation halts. Without it, the model would keep predicting forever. Every sentence you‚Äôve ever received from an LLM ended because of this token.

The Greedy Problem: Always Picking the Top Word

What if the model always chose the highest-probability word?

Prompt: ‚ÄúTell me a joke.‚Äù

Run Response
1st ‚ÄúWhy did the chicken cross the road? To get to the other side.‚Äù

The Problem

This is called greedy decoding. The model becomes a broken record ‚Äî deterministic, robotic, and utterly predictable. Every identical prompt returns the exact same output forever.

We need randomness ‚Äî but controlled randomness.

Temperature adjusts the shape of that probability curve.

Top-K simply chops off the list after \(K\) items.

Here, setting \(K=2\) forces it to stick to the very most likely words, preventing weird deviations like ‚ÄúUnicorn‚Äù.

Top-P (Nucleus): The ‚ÄúSmart‚Äù Cutoff

Top-P sums up probabilities until it hits \(P\) (e.g., 0.9).

  • Result: All three are kept.

  • Result: Hit 90% immediately. Stops there.

It adapts to the context. It‚Äôs strict when the answer is clear (Riyadh), and flexible when it‚Äôs open-ended or we have synonyms.

Hallucinations: When Guessing Fails

Hallucinations are structural. They aren‚Äôt a bug; they are the result of the model doing its job without enough context.

  1. Probability over Truth ‚Äî It picks statistically likely words, not verified facts.
  2. Plausibility over Accuracy ‚Äî It is trained to sound correct, which makes its lies dangerous.
  3. Training Gaps ‚Äî If it hasn‚Äôt seen the ‚ÄúSaudi Investment Law,‚Äù it will confidently invent one that sounds legal.

Production Risk

A hallucinating chatbot creates real legal and financial liability. ‚ÄúConfident, fluent lies‚Äù are harder to spot than obvious errors.

Mitigation Strategies

Strategy How It Works When to Use
RAG Ground responses in retrieved documents Most enterprise apps (Module 03)
Output Validation Verify against structured data/rules Factual claims, numbers
Temp = 0 Force the most likely (factual) path extraction, classification
Human-in-the-Loop Human review before action Critical decisions

The Core Lesson

The same mechanism that writes a creative novel also writes a confident lie. Control the mechanism, or provide the data.

F. Key Intuitions

What you know and what‚Äôs next

Key Takeaways

  1. GenAI generates, not retrieves ‚Äî it creates content by predicting what comes next, at scale.
  2. Completion is the primitive ‚Äî translation, coding, and conversation all reduce to next-word prediction.
  3. Scale created emergence ‚Äî GPT-3 wasn‚Äôt programmed to translate; it learned translation as a side effect of predicting text at 175B parameters.
  4. Hallucinations are structural ‚Äî the model generates plausible text, not true text. Always plan a mitigation strategy.
  5. SFT aligned completers ‚Äî ChatGPT didn‚Äôt invent a new architecture; it trained the model to treat prompts as commands.
  6. In-context learning ‚Äî you can adapt a model to new tasks in real time, just by showing examples in the prompt.
  7. Thinking models trade speed for accuracy ‚Äî chain of thought and o1-style reasoning give you better answers at higher cost and latency.
